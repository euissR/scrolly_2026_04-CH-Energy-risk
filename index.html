Europe’s energy security architecture must change as the geopolitical threat landscape evolves. As became clear with the war in the Middle East and before that the Russian induced energy shock, the EU remains painfully exposed to global energy shocks and coercion through control of energy. Increasing disruption to energy flows, both intentional and circumstantial, is only likely to increase under unpredictable geopolitics and the effects climate change. Vulnerability of infrastructure too will only become more pressing as Europe looks to defend itself against Russian aggression.

Modelling based on fuel inputs to the EU’s energy system provides numbers to the scale of the risk. This reveals that across multiple scenarios, fossil fuel inputs, notably oil and gas, are considerably more geopolitically vulnerable than renewables. The risks extend from not just dependencies and supplier concentration but also to infrastructure vulnerability. The modelling indicates that the problem is not just about supply but fundamental to the fuel inputs themselves challenging the merit in domestic fossil fuel production or new drilling in the Arctic. In all scenarios except mineral supply shocks, fossil fuels are the most exposed, in some cases by over 10 times those of all renewables. Even in mineral supply shocks effecting 90% of critical raw material imports the geopolitical risk to offshore wind for example is lower than even the baseline risk associated with LNG.

The aim of the model is to provide a quantitative assessment of the geopolitical risk faced by each fuel input into the current energy system. The results show the relative scale of geopolitical exposure faced by each fuel inputs. The model uses a scenario-based methodology based around measures of variables associated with geopolitical risk including import dependency, cost change vulnerability, supplier concentration and infrastructure exposure among others. The scenarios are illustrative of historical and possible events. They are independent of one another. The full results and methodology are in the annex.

Results by scenario

The Baseline: Not all fuels start without geopolitical risk

Oil, gas, coal and uranium come with preexisting import dependencies which are only partially negated by the market’s ability to reroute in the case of a crisis.

The supply shortages following the blocking of Hormuz, especially in jet fuel, demonstrates the limits of the global market to quickly compensate for supply shortages.

Scenario 1

A loss of 5% of supply over 30 days which would impact global flows of energy The scenario historical prece dent in oil, gas and nuclear. It has is also possible from a future OPEC+ supply reduction, longer maintenance on a UK or Norwegian gas field as well as potential mining output lost to extreme weather.

Such a relatively small supply shock over a shorter period of time are within the scope of security of supply simulations conducted by organisations such as ENTSO-G and used by the European Commission. Usually such a supply shock would be manageable in terms of supply security but the model aims to give numbers to the impacts of the shock on the EU. Even if security of supply is maintained, historical shocks have shifted leverage in foreign policy and create new dependencies.

In the case of a single supply shock, two additional criteria come into play: storage and price.

As the shock is over within 30 days, storage capacity does not feature as a multiplier of risk in this scenario as European stockpiles of all fuels are sufficient to meet demand over the timeframe.

However the import dependency and financial costs increase.

Scenario 4: [Placeholder title — e.g. "Hybrid warfare against energy infrastructure"]

[Placeholder: A paragraph framing Scenario 4 — hybrid conflict combines conventional disruptions with cyberattacks and infrastructure sabotage.]

The scenario opens with a supply shock to fossil fuel markets — the same baseline disruption as Scenarios 1 and 2.

Storage losses follow, as energy infrastructure comes under pressure — a characteristic feature of hybrid conflict affecting gas networks in particular.

Simultaneously, price shocks and mineral supply disruptions constrain both fossil fuel alternatives and renewable deployment, leaving fewer substitutes available.

Hybrid warfare — including cyberattacks, sabotage and sustained economic coercion — adds a final layer of risk, raising the total index to its highest level across all fuels.

Like Scenario 4, this begins with a supply shock to fossil fuel markets — establishing the pre-conflict baseline exposure.

Storage infrastructure is damaged or destroyed in the early phase of conflict, removing critical buffers that otherwise moderate price volatility.

Price and mineral shocks cascade across the energy system simultaneously, constraining the scope for substitution.

Direct kinetic strikes on energy infrastructure produce the highest risk levels of any scenario assessed — with physical destruction causing cascading failures across fossil fuel and low-carbon systems alike.
