Europe’s energy security architecture must change as the geopolitical threat landscape evolves. As became clear with the war in the Middle East and before that the Russian induced energy shock, the EU remains painfully exposed to global energy shocks and coercion through control of energy. Increasing disruption to energy flows, both intentional and circumstantial, is only likely to increase under unpredictable geopolitics and the effects climate change. Vulnerability of infrastructure too will only become more pressing as Europe looks to defend itself against Russian aggression.

Modelling based on fuel inputs to the EU’s energy system provides numbers to the scale of the risk. This reveals that across multiple scenarios, fossil fuel inputs, notably oil and gas, are considerably more geopolitically vulnerable than renewables. The risks extend from not just dependencies and supplier concentration but also to infrastructure vulnerability. The modelling indicates that the problem is not just about supply but fundamental to the fuel inputs themselves challenging the merit in domestic fossil fuel production or new drilling in the Arctic. In all scenarios except mineral supply shocks, fossil fuels are the most exposed, in some cases by over 10 times those of all renewables. Even in mineral supply shocks effecting 90% of critical raw material imports the geopolitical risk to offshore wind for example is lower than even the baseline risk associated with LNG.

The aim of the model is to provide a quantitative assessment of the geopolitical risk faced by each fuel input into the current energy system. The results show the relative scale of geopolitical exposure faced by each fuel inputs. The model uses a scenario-based methodology based around measures of variables associated with geopolitical risk including import dependency, cost change vulnerability, supplier concentration and infrastructure exposure among others. The scenarios are illustrative of historical and possible events. They are independent of one another. The full results and methodology are in the annex.

Results by scenario

The Baseline: Not all fuels start without geopolitical risk

Oil, gas, coal and uranium come with preexisting import dependencies which are only partially negated by the market’s ability to reroute in the case of a crisis.

The supply shortages following the blocking of Hormuz, especially in jet fuel, demonstrates the limits of the global market to quickly compensate for supply shortages.

Scenario 1

A loss of 5% of supply over 30 days which would impact global flows of energy The scenario historical prece dent in oil, gas and nuclear. It has is also possible from a future OPEC+ supply reduction, longer maintenance on a UK or Norwegian gas field as well as potential mining output lost to extreme weather.

Such a relatively small supply shock over a shorter period of time are within the scope of security of supply simulations conducted by organisations such as ENTSO-G and used by the European Commission. Usually such a supply shock would be manageable in terms of supply security but the model aims to give numbers to the impacts of the shock on the EU. Even if security of supply is maintained, historical shocks have shifted leverage in foreign policy and create new dependencies.

In the case of a single supply shock, two additional criteria come into play: storage and price.

As the shock is over within 30 days, storage capacity does not feature as a multiplier of risk in this scenario as European stockpiles of all fuels are sufficient to meet demand over the timeframe.

However the import dependency and financial costs increase.

Scenario 2

Scenario 2 looks at a similar supply shock but more intense and from multiple points of supply. Again this is feasible with one or more points of failure in the fuel supply chain. A cumulative loss of oil and gas from the straits of Hormuz for example could be coupled with lost output in the US either by intent or from extreme weather in a hurricane prone region.

The scenario assumes 10% supply lost but over 90 days.

In the case of a multiple supply shock over 90 days, storage does become a problem notably for gas. The model considers that pressure would increase per day that supply is disrupted and storage has run out. This assumption is based on previous energy supply shocks including in the 1970s and 2022.

The limited number of suppliers also increases the vulnerability of supply chains making the supplier concentration more important in this scenario too.

The tightness in the market would also likely have an increased price pain point. Such price spikes have followed major supply disruptions including 2022 and 2026 for oil, gas and to a lesser extent coal and for Uranium in 2007.

Scenario 3

Scenario 3 simulates a major supply shock to mineral supply chains used in the energy system over 90 days. Given the concentration in the market especially on China, a major supply shock of 90% is possible. China has already employed economic coercion through use of the critical raw materials trade weapon. While the consequences of this action would be considerable, the model only considers the impact on the current energy system.

The model takes a basket of minerals including rare earths, copper, nickel and others and weighs them against the mineral intensity per megawatt hour of electricity generation.

While there is some impact but the effect on the existing system is limited. Only offshore wind shows some change.

No fuel input would be completely shielded though as all require some degree of mineral input from China.

The costs would also be felt across all fuels but in a uniform way

The relative hit to the system though is small compared to supply shocks as the existing assets continue to produce electricity even if newer assets would be hard to add. The model therefore shows the advantage of establishing energy assets before a supply shock which themselves make the system more resistant to future shocks.

Scenario 4

Scenario 4 considers a medium intensity hybrid conflict. It factors in the cumulative effects of a supply shock alongside assumptions about the effects on energy infrastructure.

Such a scenario could play out especially in the case of escalation by the Russian federation. It has shown willingness to disrupt energy flows and has launched physical attacks on infrastructure including cables.

In April 2026, British officials uncovered a Russian covert operation over undersea cables and pipelines, with the intention of harm.

EU system operators too have seen a rise in more coordinated criminal attacks on infrastructure which aligns with the Kremlin’s use of local actors to carry out hybrid attacks.

A coordinated strike could have severe impacts on the energy system as modelled.

First a small supply and storage shock which would have a slightly reduced market rebalancing effect.

There would also be a related cost shock.

This is complemented by strikes on the energy infrastructure in the cyber and physical domains.

The cumulative risk to the energy system increases considerably but unevenly.

Centralised and vulnerable pieces of infrastructure including undersea cables, compressor stations and substations are disproportionately affected. These have ripple effects on each fuel, but some, including nuclear and gas are hit more than others.

The central role played by gas in the electricity system for example shows it is particularly vulnerable to impact from targeted strikes.

Cyber impacts, an important feature of the hybrid scenario, are deemed more risky in some fuels, especially solar. Nevertheless the broader impact of cyber attacks are quite evenly spread as the points of highest impact are system wide (e.g SCADA systems in control centres)

Scenario 5

Scenario 5 measures the effects of a medium intensity kinetic conflict on the energy system. It is an imagined scenario as Europe’s energy system has not had to deal with such an event before. The model exposes the vulnerabilities especially to military relevant infrastructure.

It is also cumulative with an assumed supply disruption and storage stress although the market reaction forces are reduced.

The length of time, 100 days, increases the intensity of the supply and storage shock.

The cost shock remains but its relative importance is reduced. The model considers both fuel and mineral supply lost.

The major addition though is the increased intensity of the conflict, broader range of infrastructure attacked and the large increase in importance of military relevant inputs.

This is particularly striking in the vulnerability of oil and petroleum products which would be in high demand for the military in a kinetic conflict.

This in turn would increase the vulnerability of centralised processing infrastructure such as refineries.

As a result it is the single most geopolitically exposed fuel in a war scenario.

Gas too suffers disproportionately as its centralised infrastructure assets (gas storage sites are not considered at risk) would be highly vulnerable to drone and missile strikes. This is a similar problem for coal and to a lesser extent hydropower.

Nuclear is assumed to have a degree of immunity from direct strikes given the risks associated with an attack .

Renewables, especially wind and solar, are not immune but considerably less effected.

The distributed nature of the resources on onshore wind and solar is a major strength while they remain free of major supply and storage constraints. The remaining risk is from mineral supply disruptions and collateral damage to the electricity system.

Recommendations

Gas, especially LNG, is highly exposed to geopolitical risk factors. Its baseline risk without weighting is over 0.5X more exposed than renewables rising to 2X and 5X in supply shock scenarios then 10X in hybrid and 10X in kinetic war. There should be an urgent prioritisation to reducing the importance of gas in the energy system. By dramatically reducing the relevance of gas in the energy system, the risk is reduced. The clearest route is for electrification of heating, a major pain point. It is also vital to reduce its role in the electricity system, although difficult, this would also reduce some of the pain points notably in decreasing the value of gas infrastructure as a target.

Oil and Petroleum products are similarly highly exposed especially given their large import dependencies. It is 1.6X to 1.7X more exposed than renewables in supply shock scenarios rising to 6X in hybrid and 10X in kinetic war. They are particularly exposed in a hybrid or kinetic scenario given their importance to sustaining the military architecture. Demand is sticky making quick reductions difficult. One of the main problems is military demand which accounts for a major part of the risk in the kinetic war scenarios. Priority should be to reduce demand especially through the development of liquid fuels for military aviation.

Renewables, wind solar and hydro, are consistently the least exposed to geopolitical shocks. Even where the percentage of renewables increase in the system dramatically, they still retain a strong geopolitical advantage over other fuels. Importantly this is not linear as solar and wind alone cannot support the electricity system but in combination with distributed storage it offers a much more resilient fuel input. A move to a renewables based system would require a change in demand through electrification, also of military supply chains.

The main vulnerabilities in wind and solar are in cyber and supply chains. Both of these can be at least partially negated through building stronger Europe and partner supply chains and stronger cyber security protocols. Both are more systemic problems than fuel supply creating incentive for the development of cross-sector resilience .

Nuclear is retains considerable geopolitically exposure. While the scale of the supply shocks are not perfectly aligned with the uranium market, there is considerable supplier concentration and vulnerability within the supply chains. It strength therefore lies primarily in the long storage times of existing reserves as well as norms against targeting of nuclear energy infrastructure. Neither are guaranteed points of resilience in an uncertain world

Coal has relative resilience due to some existing domestic supply and strong storage potential in particular. However it still retains considerable infrastructure risks associated with centralised infrastructure which makes it much more vulnerable in hybrid and kinetic war scenarios. The model also does not measure climate risks which are more pronounced from coal than from even other fossil fuels.
